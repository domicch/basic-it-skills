# Internet Safety for Kids

The internet is amazing. You can watch videos, message friends, search for anything you want to learn, and discover cool things. But the internet also has risks. This course teaches you how to be smart online and protect yourself.

## Why Internet Safety Matters

When you use the internet, you're connecting with millions of people all over the world. Most people are good. But some people are not nice. Some people try to trick you, steal your information, or hurt you.

By learning how to spot danger, you can enjoy the internet safely.

## Part 1: Strangers Online (Catfishing & Predators)

### Why Some People Pretend to Be Someone They're Not

On the internet, nobody knows who you really are. Some bad people use this to trick kids.

**What they do:**
- Pretend to be kids your age
- Pretend to be famous people
- Pretend to like the same things you like
- Try to become your friend
- Ask you personal questions
- Eventually ask you to send photos or meet them in person

This is called **catfishing** ‚Äî when someone pretends to be someone else to trick you.

### Red Flags: How to Spot a Fake Stranger

**Warning sign #1: They ask personal questions**

Examples:
- "What's your real name?"
- "What school do you go to?"
- "What's your address?"
- "What's your phone number?"
- "Do your parents know you're online?"
- "Can we talk on WhatsApp instead?"

**Real friends don't ask these things. If someone asks, they're suspicious.**

**Warning sign #2: Their story doesn't add up**

Examples:
- They say they're 11 years old but use complicated adult words
- They say they live in your city but don't know any landmarks
- They say they go to your school but can't name any teachers
- They say they like the same interests but don't know details

**If someone's story seems wrong, trust your instincts. They might be fake.**

**Warning sign #3: They want to move to private chat**

Examples:
- "Let's chat on WhatsApp instead of here"
- "Can you add me to WhatsApp? My parents monitor this"
- "Let's move this conversation somewhere private"

**Why they do this:** So nobody can see what they're saying. That's a huge red flag.

**Warning sign #4: They ask for photos**

Examples:
- "Send me a selfie"
- "Can I see what you look like?"
- "Send a photo of you"
- "Send a video call"

**NEVER do this. Ever. Even if they seem nice.**

**Warning sign #5: They ask to meet you**

Examples:
- "Let's meet up at the park"
- "Can I come to your house?"
- "Let's meet after school"

**NEVER agree to this. Ever.**

### Real Examples From YouTube, WhatsApp, and Websites

**Example 1: YouTube Stranger**

You post a comment on a YouTube video: "Cool video! I like gaming too."

A stranger replies: "Hey! I like gaming too! I'm 11 like you. Want to be friends? Add me on WhatsApp."

They send you their phone number.

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó They responded to your comment very quickly
- ‚úó They claim to be your age
- ‚úó They immediately want to move to WhatsApp
- ‚úó They want your phone number

**What to do:** Don't respond. Block them. Tell an adult.

**Example 2: WhatsApp from Unknown Number**

You get a WhatsApp message from a number you don't recognize:

"Hi! I'm Sophia, 11 years old. A friend showed me your YouTube comment and I thought you seemed cool. Do you want to be friends? What's your real name? What school do you go to?"

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Unknown number
- ‚úó Claims to be your age
- ‚úó Asking for your real name
- ‚úó Asking what school you go to
- ‚úó Moved to WhatsApp (you didn't even know them)

**What to do:** Don't reply. Delete the message. Block the number. Tell an adult immediately.

**Example 3: Website Chat**

You're on a website and a chat box pops up: "Hi! We're so glad you're here! What's your name? Where are you from?"

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Random website asking personal questions
- ‚úó Being too friendly too quickly
- ‚úó Asking for identifying information

**What to do:** Close the chat. Don't answer. Tell an adult.

**Example 4: YouTube Comment Pretending to Be a Friend**

You comment on a YouTube video: "This is awesome! I learned so much!"

Someone replies: "Hey! I'm Marcus from your class! I didn't know you watched this. Add me on WhatsApp!"

But you don't know anyone named Marcus at school.

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Pretending to know you
- ‚úó Claiming to be from your school
- ‚úó Wanting to move to WhatsApp
- ‚úó You've never heard of them

**What to do:** Don't reply. Block them. Tell an adult.

**Example 5: The "Free Gift Card" Scammer**

Someone messages you on WhatsApp: "Congratulations! You won a free $50 gift card! Just send me a photo of yourself and your address so I can send it to you!"

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó You never entered a contest
- ‚úó Asking for a photo
- ‚úó Asking for your address
- ‚úó Something you didn't win

**What to do:** Don't respond. Block them. Tell an adult.

### The Golden Rule: Never Share Personal Information

These things are SAFE to share online:
- ‚úì Your hobbies and interests
- ‚úì Your opinions about public topics
- ‚úì Things you learned
- ‚úì Your favorite movies or books
- ‚úì Compliments and friendly comments

These things are DANGEROUS to share:
- ‚úó Your real name (especially last name)
- ‚úó Your school name
- ‚úó Your address
- ‚úó Your phone number
- ‚úó Your parents' names or phone numbers
- ‚úó Your birthdate
- ‚úó Photos of yourself
- ‚úó Videos of yourself
- ‚úó Any password

### What to Do If Someone Makes You Uncomfortable

**Step 1: Stop talking to them immediately**

**Step 2: Block them**
- On YouTube: Click the three dots on their comment ‚Üí "Block user"
- On WhatsApp: Long-press their message ‚Üí "Block contact"
- On a website: Close the window or leave the website

**Step 3: Tell an adult**
- Tell a parent, guardian, teacher, or trusted adult
- Show them the messages
- Don't be embarrassed ‚Äî this happens to lots of people
- Adults know how to handle this

**Step 4: Report them**
- Report the account to YouTube, WhatsApp, or the website
- This helps protect other kids

## Part 2: Fake Websites & Scams

### What is a Fake Website?

A fake website is designed to trick you. It might:
- Look like a real website but have a slightly different address
- Steal your information
- Give your computer a virus
- Take you to a scam page
- Download malware onto your device

**Scammers make fake websites because they want:**
- Your personal information
- Your parents' credit card information
- To infect your computer with a virus
- To trick you into downloading something dangerous

### How to Spot a Fake Website

**Red Flag #1: The Address (URL) is Wrong**

The address bar shows the website's real name.

**Safe addresses:**
- `https://www.youtube.com` ‚úì
- `https://www.google.com` ‚úì
- `https://www.wikipedia.org` ‚úì

**Fake addresses (watch for these):**
- `https://www.youltube.com` ‚ùå (typo: "youltube" instead of "youtube")
- `https://www.ytube.com` ‚ùå (missing letters)
- `https://youtube.secure-login.com` ‚ùå (extra words added)
- `https://www.youtube.com-verify.net` ‚ùå (sneaky misspelling)
- `https://youtubedownload.site` ‚ùå (wrong domain)

**How to check:**
1. Look at the address bar at the top
2. Read it carefully ‚Äî letter by letter
3. If it's even slightly different, it's FAKE

**Red Flag #2: No "https" or Padlock Icon**

Every safe website has:
- `https` at the beginning (not just `http`)
- A padlock icon üîí next to the address

If you don't see these, the website is NOT SAFE.

**Red Flag #3: Poor Spelling and Grammar**

Real websites are made by companies that check their work.

Fake websites often have:
- Misspelled words
- Bad grammar
- Weird sentences

**Example of fake text:**
"Congrats! You are the lucky winner! Click here for claim you prize!!!"

(Notice: "claim you prize" should be "claim your prize")

**Real text:**
"Congratulations! You won the drawing. Click here to claim your prize."

**Red Flag #4: Offers That Are Too Good to Be True**

**Fake offers:**
- "FREE iPhone! Click here!"
- "You won $10,000 instantly!"
- "Unlimited free movie downloads!"
- "Free gift card ‚Äî no purchase needed!"
- "You were selected as our 1,000th visitor!"

**The truth:** Real companies don't give away expensive things for free. If it sounds too good to be true, it IS too good to be true.

**Red Flag #5: Asking for Personal Information**

**Fake websites might ask:**
- "Enter your parents' credit card number"
- "What's your real name and address?"
- "What's your school name?"
- "Confirm your phone number"

**Real websites don't ask for:**
- Your address (just for shipping)
- Your parents' credit card (unless buying something)
- Your school name
- Your phone number (unless necessary)

### Real Examples of Fake Websites

**Example 1: Fake YouTube Download Site**

You watch a YouTube video and see a comment: "Download this video for free here! [link]"

You click the link. The website looks kind of like YouTube, but:
- The address is `www.youtubedownload.xyz` (NOT youtube.com)
- There's no padlock icon
- It says "Download Now!" in big red letters
- It asks for your parents' credit card number

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Wrong address
- ‚úó No padlock
- ‚úó Asking for credit card
- ‚úó Looks similar but different

**What to do:** Leave immediately. Don't download anything. Tell an adult.

**Example 2: Fake "You Won a Prize!" Website**

A popup appears while you're browsing: "CONGRATULATIONS! YOU'RE OUR 1,000TH VISITOR! YOU WON AN iPAD! CLICK HERE!"

You click it. The website asks:
- "What's your full name?"
- "What's your address?"
- "What's your parents' email?"

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó You never entered a contest
- ‚úó The website looks cheap/poorly made
- ‚úó Asking for lots of personal info
- ‚úó Poor spelling and grammar
- ‚úó Address looks wrong

**What to do:** Close the browser tab immediately. Don't fill out any form. Tell an adult.

**Example 3: Fake Free Movie Website**

You search Google for "free movies online" and find a website called "MoviesFree.net".

The website has:
- Address: `http://moviefree.net` (no "https", no padlock)
- Lots of spelling mistakes
- Ads everywhere
- A big button saying "Click to Download FREE!"
- Asks you to enter your birthday

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó No https or padlock
- ‚úó Poor design and spelling
- ‚úó Too many ads
- ‚úó Asking for personal info
- ‚úó Offering something for free that costs money

**What to do:** Leave the website. Don't click anything. Tell an adult.

**Example 4: Fake School Login**

You get an email that looks like it's from your school: "Click here to access your school portal. Enter your username and password."

You click the link. The website looks like your school's website, but:
- The address is `www.school-portal.net` (NOT your real school's address)
- There's no padlock icon
- The design looks slightly off

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Email asking you to click and log in
- ‚úó Wrong address
- ‚úó No padlock
- ‚úó Design looks slightly wrong

**What to do:** Don't log in. Tell an adult. Your real school will never ask you to log in through an email.

**Example 5: Fake Download Button**

You're on a website and see a big button that says "DOWNLOAD" but it's NOT from the actual website.

For example, you want to download a video and there are THREE "Download" buttons on the page. One is from the real website (small, on the side). Two are FAKE (big, in the middle, in different colors).

**Red flags:**
- ‚úó Multiple download buttons
- ‚úó Buttons that look like ads
- ‚úó Buttons in weird places

**What to do:** Use only the official download button. If you're not sure which one is real, ask an adult.

### How to Verify a Real Website

**Method 1: Check the Address**
1. Look at the address bar
2. Read it carefully ‚Äî every letter
3. Make sure it matches the real website name
4. Look for the padlock icon

**Method 2: Use Google**
1. Close the website
2. Open Google
3. Search for the official website name
4. Click the first result from Google
5. Now you're on the real website

**Method 3: Ask an Adult**
If you're not sure if a website is real, ask a parent or trusted adult before clicking anything.

## Part 3: Protecting Your Personal Information

### What is Personal Information?

Personal information is anything that identifies you:
- Your real name
- Your address
- Your school
- Your phone number
- Your birthdate
- Photos of you
- Videos of you
- Your parents' information

### Information That's SAFE to Share

These things are okay to share online:

‚úì Your hobbies and interests ("I like reading and science")
‚úì Your opinions ("This movie was amazing!")
‚úì Things you learned ("Today I learned about dinosaurs")
‚úì Your favorite books, movies, or games
‚úì Compliments and friendly comments
‚úì Your thoughts on public topics

### Information That's DANGEROUS to Share

**NEVER share:**

| Information | Why It's Dangerous |
|-------------|-------------------|
| **Your real name** | Someone could find your social media and learn more about you |
| **Your last name** | Combined with your first name, people can locate you |
| **Your school name** | Someone could find you in person |
| **Your address** | Burglars could rob your house; stalkers could find you |
| **Your phone number** | Scammers could call you or your parents; harassing calls |
| **Your birthdate** | Used for identity theft; predators use it to build trust |
| **Photos of you** | Could be used to catfish other kids; could be shared anywhere |
| **Your parents' names** | Scammers could impersonate them; stalkers could find them |
| **Your parents' phone number** | Scammers could call them |
| **Your passwords** | Someone could hack your accounts |
| **Your location** | Someone could find where you are right now |

### Real Examples of Information Being Misused

**Example 1: Name + School = Found in Person**

You comment on YouTube with your real name: "Great video! I go to Lincoln Elementary School!"

A stranger reads this. They:
- Search for your name online
- Find your school
- Find your Facebook or other social media
- Learn when you go to school
- Wait for you at the school gate

**How to prevent this:** Never share your school name online.

**Example 2: Address = Home Address Shared**

You fill out an online form with your address because a website promised a "free prize".

The website wasn't real. Your address is now:
- Sold to scammers
- Used to send mail scams
- Used by someone to find your house
- Used by thieves who know when you're home

**How to prevent this:** Never give your address to unknown websites.

**Example 3: Birthdate = Identity Theft Risk**

You share your birthdate online: "Just turned 11 today!"

A scammer now has:
- Your name
- Your birthdate
- Your school

They could:
- Pretend to be you
- Open fake accounts in your name
- Try to access your email
- Commit fraud using your identity

**How to prevent this:** Never share your birthdate online.

**Example 4: Photo = Used for Catfishing**

You send a selfie to someone you met online.

That person:
- Saves your photo
- Uses it to catfish OTHER kids
- Posts it on fake profiles
- Shares it without your permission

**How to prevent this:** Never send photos to people online, ever.

**Example 5: Location = Stalking Risk**

You post on YouTube: "Just got home from school!"

A person with bad intentions knows:
- Where you are (home)
- When you're home (right now)
- Maybe even your address

**How to prevent this:** Never share where you are in real time.

### Where Your Information Gets Shared

**YouTube Comments**
- Public to everyone on the internet
- Saved forever (even if you delete them)
- Can be screenshot or saved

**Example:**
You comment: "My name is Sarah and I go to Lincoln School"
‚úó Now anyone on the internet knows this

**WhatsApp Messages**
- Saved on the person's phone
- Can be screenshot
- Can be shared with others
- Screenshot might include your number

**Example:**
You message someone: "Hi, I'm Sarah, I live on Oak Street"
‚úó They can screenshot this and send it to anyone

**Website Forms**
- Information goes to the website owner
- Might be sold to scammers
- Might be hacked
- You have no control over it

**Example:**
You fill out: Name, Address, Phone Number, Email
‚úó All this information is now in their database

**Location Services on Apps**
- Apps can track where you are
- Apps can share your location with others
- Your location history is recorded

**Example:**
You turn on location for a YouTube app
‚úó The app knows where you are at all times

### How to Protect Your Personal Information

**Rule #1: Think Before You Share**

Before posting or commenting, ask yourself:
- Would I tell this information to a stranger?
- Could this information be used to hurt me?
- Could someone find me or my house with this?
- Could this information follow me forever?

If you answer YES to any of these, DON'T share it.

**Rule #2: Use Privacy Settings**

If you use YouTube or other websites:
- Keep your real name private
- Don't fill out personal information in profiles
- Don't share your location
- Don't friend/follow strangers

**Rule #3: Turn Off Location Services**

Location services let apps know where you are.
- Turn off location for apps you don't need it for
- Only turn it on for maps if you're lost
- Ask an adult how to do this

**Rule #4: Check Forms Before Submitting**

Before clicking "Submit" on any website form:
- ‚úì Is this website real? (Check the address)
- ‚úì Do I recognize this website?
- ‚úì Does this website really need this information?
- ‚úì Can I trust this website?

If you're not sure, ask an adult.

**Rule #5: Tell an Adult if You're Unsure**

When in doubt, ask a parent, guardian, or trusted adult:
- "Is this website real?"
- "Should I share this information?"
- "Does this seem like a scam?"

### Real Examples of Protecting Your Information

**Example 1: Safe YouTube Comment**

‚ùå Bad comment: "My name is Sarah and I live on Oak Street in Boston and go to Lincoln School"

‚úì Good comment: "Great video! I learned a lot about dinosaurs!"

The good comment doesn't share any personal information.

**Example 2: Safe WhatsApp**

‚ùå Bad message: "Hi! My name is Sarah, I'm 11, I go to Lincoln School, and I live on Oak Street"

‚úì Good message: "Hi! I saw your YouTube video and loved it!"

The good message doesn't share identifying information.

**Example 3: Suspicious Website Form**

A website asks: "Name, Address, Phone Number, Birthday"

You check:
- ‚úó Address is wrong (not https)
- ‚úó Website is unknown
- ‚úó Why do they need my phone number?
- ‚úó This seems like a scam

‚úì You don't fill it out. You tell an adult.

**Example 4: Website You Recognize**

A real website you know asks: "Name and Address (for shipping)"

You check:
- ‚úì Address is correct (https and padlock)
- ‚úì This is a website you know
- ‚úì They only need name and address (for shipping)
- ‚úì You're buying something

‚úì This is probably safe. You can share name and address.

## Key Takeaways

### Remember These Golden Rules

‚úì **Never share personal information with strangers online**
‚úì **Never send photos or videos to people you don't know**
‚úì **Never agree to meet someone you met online**
‚úì **Always check if a website is real** (https, padlock, correct address)
‚úì **Never trust offers that seem too good to be true**
‚úì **Always tell an adult if something makes you uncomfortable**
‚úì **Block anyone who bothers you**
‚úì **If you're unsure about something, ask an adult**

### Internet Safety Checklist

Before you browse the internet, ask yourself:

- [ ] Am I sharing personal information? (Name, address, school, phone)
- [ ] Am I talking to a stranger? (Unknown person online)
- [ ] Does this website look real? (Check the address and padlock)
- [ ] Is this offer too good to be true? (Free phones, free money, free gifts)
- [ ] Am I comfortable with this? (Or does it seem wrong?)
- [ ] Have I told an adult what I'm doing online?

If anything makes you uncomfortable or suspicious, STOP and tell an adult.

### What To Do If Something Goes Wrong

**If someone bothers you:**
1. Block them
2. Don't respond to them
3. Tell an adult immediately
4. Report them to YouTube, WhatsApp, or the website

**If you see a fake website:**
1. Leave the website immediately
2. Don't download anything
3. Don't enter any information
4. Tell an adult
5. Report the website

**If you accidentally shared information:**
1. Tell an adult immediately
2. Change your passwords
3. Don't panic ‚Äî adults can help
